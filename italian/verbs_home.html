Conjugations

There are three conjugations of verbs in Italian, and each has different endings as infinitives.

  • First Conjugation (-are) example: parlare to speak
  • Second Conjugation (-ere) example: credere to believe
  • Third Conjugation (-ire) example: dormire to sleep

Past Tense

There are multiple past tenses in Italian:

The Present Perfect (Il passato prossimo) describes single actions/events or changes in state in the past. Present Perfect uses the helping verbs avere or essere along with the past participle of the verb. There is more information on the Present Perfect tense below.

The Imperfect (L'imperfetto) is used to describe habits or repeated actions not happening at a specific time. L'imperfetto combines past tense with imperfect aspect ( actions not viewed as a complete whole). L'imperfetto is used to describe:

  • repeated or habitual actions in the past
  • ongoing actions in the past and ongiong actions in the past that are interrupted
  • states of being and conditions in the past (Example: weather, time, age)

First Conjugation

Present Tense First Conjugation Endings (with parlare)
io parlo noi parliamo
tu parli voi parlate
lui/lei/Lei parla loro/Loro parlano

Il Passato Prossimo

All transitive verbs and most intransitive verbs use the helping verb avere (to have) when forming their Present Perfect. For verbs that use avere as an auxiliary, the past participle will almost always end in -o. (Example: amato)

Here is how the past participle is formed in each conjugation:

  • First conjugation -are verbs add -ato to the stem
  • Second conjugation -ere verbs add -uto to the stem
  • Third conjugation -ire verbs add -ito to the stem

Present Perfect Tense avere auxiliary (with amare)
io ho amato noi abbiamo amato
tu hai amato voi avete amato
lui/lei/Lei ha amato loro/Loro hanno amato

A few intransitive verbs (essere itself) and verbs that indicate motion (to come, to go, to arrive, etc.) use essere as their auxiliary verb. When this is the case, the past participle will need to agree with the number and gender of the subject. The verb arrivare (to arrive) in the example below uses essere as an auxiliary: It is conjugated as if the subject is masculine. The past participle arrivato change to arrivata in the singular and would change from arrivati to arrivate in the plural in the case of a feminine subject such as penna (pen).

Present Perfect Tense essere auxiliary (with arrivare)
io sono arrivato noi siamo arrivati
tu sei arrivato voi siete arrivati
lui/lei/Lei è arrivato loro/Loro sono arrivati
Once again, -o will become -a and -i will become -e
